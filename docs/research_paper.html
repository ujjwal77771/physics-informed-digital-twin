Abstract

Accurate prediction of remaining useful life (RUL) is critical for condition-based maintenance of rotating machinery, yet purely data-driven models frequently produce physically inconsistent degradation trajectories. This paper presents VibFormer, a physics-informed digital twin framework that unifies a patch-based Transformer encoder with domain-specific loss regularization grounded in Hertzian contact mechanics, Palmgren–Miner fatigue accumulation, and Paris' crack-propagation law. The architecture processes multi-sensor vibration telemetry from Rexnord ZA-2115 bearings via temporal patch projection and multi-head self-attention, while a composite loss function enforces monotonic health-index decay and physics-consistent degradation rates. Deployed as an end-to-end digital twin—comprising an ONNX-optimized inference backend served through FastAPI, real-time WebSocket telemetry streaming, and a React/Three.js dashboard with 3D bearing visualization—VibFormer achieves an RMSE of 12.87 cycles and a NASA Score of 241.3 on the IMS bearing dataset, outperforming LSTM, CNN, and MLP baselines by 18–39% while maintaining a monotonicity score of 0.97. Ablation studies confirm that each physics-informed component contributes meaningfully to prediction quality and physical plausibility.

Keywords: Remaining useful life, physics-informed neural network, digital twin, transformer, bearing prognostics, predictive maintenance, Hertzian contact stress, self-attention.

I. Introduction

Rolling element bearings constitute the most failure-prone components in rotating industrial machinery, accounting for approximately 40–50% of all electric motor failures and an estimated 30% of gearbox failures across the manufacturing, energy, and transportation sectors[1]. The consequences of unplanned bearing failure extend far beyond the cost of the replacement part itself: a single unscheduled shutdown on a continuous-process production line can incur losses ranging from $10,000 to over $250,000 per hour, depending on the industry vertical[2]. In safety-critical applications such as wind turbine drivetrains, railway axle-boxes, and aerospace engine accessories, bearing failure can escalate into catastrophic secondary damage or endanger human life.

Condition-based maintenance (CBM) and predictive maintenance (PdM) strategies aim to mitigate these risks by monitoring the operational health of equipment in real-time and scheduling maintenance interventions only when degradation indicators suggest imminent failure. Central to any PdM strategy is the estimation of remaining useful life (RUL)—the time or number of operational cycles remaining before a component can no longer perform its intended function within acceptable performance bounds. Accurate RUL prediction enables maintenance planners to balance the competing objectives of maximizing asset utilization and minimizing the risk of unplanned downtime, thereby reducing total lifecycle costs by an estimated 25–30% compared to fixed-interval preventive maintenance schedules[3].

Traditional approaches to bearing RUL prediction fall broadly into three categories: physics-based (model-driven), data-driven, and hybrid methods. Physics-based models, grounded in contact mechanics, fracture mechanics, and fatigue theory, offer interpretable degradation trajectories but require precise knowledge of material properties, loading conditions, and failure modes that are rarely available in operational settings[4]. Data-driven methods, including recurrent neural networks (RNNs), convolutional neural networks (CNNs), and more recently Transformer architectures, have demonstrated strong performance on benchmark datasets but suffer from well-documented failure modes: non-monotonic degradation curves, overconfident extrapolation in data-sparse regimes, and brittleness under distributional shift[5]. Hybrid approaches that embed physical knowledge as inductive bias within data-driven architectures represent a promising direction, yet existing work has primarily focused on simple penalty terms without systematically integrating the governing equations of bearing degradation physics.

Concurrently, the digital twin paradigm has emerged as a transformative framework for lifecycle management of physical assets. A digital twin maintains a continuously-updated virtual representation of a physical system, ingesting sensor data in real-time, running predictive models against the current state, and providing actionable maintenance intelligence through interactive visualization dashboards[6]. While the conceptual appeal is clear, practical implementations of digital twins for bearing prognostics remain rare due to the engineering complexity of integrating physics-informed inference, real-time data pipelines, and production-grade deployment infrastructure into a cohesive system.

This paper addresses these gaps through the following contributions:

  • VibFormer Architecture: A patch-based Transformer encoder specifically designed for multi-sensor vibration time series, incorporating learnable positional embeddings and global average pooling for RUL regression.
  • Physics-Informed Loss Function: A composite training objective that combines standard data-driven loss with physics residuals derived from Paris' crack-propagation law and a monotonicity constraint that enforces thermodynamically consistent degradation trajectories.
  • End-to-End Digital Twin System: A complete deployment pipeline from raw sensor data to interactive dashboard, featuring ONNX Runtime inference, FastAPI backend with WebSocket streaming, SQLite time-series storage, and a React/Three.js frontend with 3D bearing visualization.
  • Comprehensive Evaluation: Quantitative benchmarking against LSTM, CNN, and MLP baselines on the IMS bearing dataset, together with ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each physics-informed component.

II. Background and Related Work

A. Traditional RUL Methods

Early bearing prognostic methods relied on vibration-based condition monitoring with manually defined alarm thresholds. Spectral analysis techniques—particularly envelope analysis and high-frequency resonance techniques—could identify characteristic bearing defect frequencies (BPFO, BPFI, BSF, FTF) but offered limited predictive capability regarding time-to-failure[7]. Statistical models, including Weibull analysis and proportional hazards models, provided probabilistic failure distributions from historical run-to-failure data but could not adapt to the real-time degradation trajectory of an individual bearing. Kalman filtering and particle filtering approaches offered recursive state estimation of degradation models but required careful specification of the state-transition dynamics and measurement models, limiting their applicability in complex multi-mode degradation scenarios[8].

B. Data-Driven Approaches

The advent of deep learning catalyzed a paradigm shift in prognostics. Recurrent architectures, particularly Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs), became the dominant approach for RUL prediction due to their natural ability to model temporal dependencies in sequential sensor data[9]. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) were subsequently applied to both raw vibration waveforms and time-frequency representations (spectrograms), demonstrating that spatial feature hierarchies could capture degradation patterns that recurrent models might miss[10]. Hybrid CNN-LSTM architectures combined spatial feature extraction with temporal modeling, achieving improved performance on benchmark datasets such as C-MAPSS and PRONOSTIA[11]. However, these purely data-driven approaches share fundamental limitations: they treat degradation as an unconstrained regression problem, resulting in predictions that may violate basic physical principles—such as predicting that a degrading bearing's health is improving—particularly when the model encounters operating conditions or degradation stages underrepresented in the training data.

C. Physics-Informed Neural Networks

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), as formalized by Raissi et al.[12], introduced the concept of embedding partial differential equation (PDE) residuals directly into neural network loss functions. This approach constrains the solution space to functions that approximately satisfy the governing physics, reducing data requirements and improving generalization. In the prognostics domain, Yucesan and Viana[13] applied PINNs to wind turbine main bearing fatigue, encoding the Palmgren–Miner damage accumulation rule as a physics constraint. Zhang et al.[14] proposed a physics-informed recurrent network that incorporated monotonicity constraints for battery RUL prediction. Nascimento and Viana[15] demonstrated fleet-level prognostics using cumulative damage models as physics priors. Despite these advances, the systematic integration of bearing-specific physics—Hertzian contact theory, rolling contact fatigue, and crack propagation mechanics—into modern Transformer architectures remains underexplored.

D. Transformers in Prognostics

The Transformer architecture, originally developed for natural language processing[16], has recently been adapted for time-series forecasting and prognostics. The self-attention mechanism enables direct modeling of long-range temporal dependencies without the sequential processing bottleneck of recurrent architectures. Li et al.[17] proposed a Transformer-based approach for turbofan engine RUL prediction using the C-MAPSS dataset, demonstrating competitive performance with significantly reduced training time compared to LSTM baselines. Mo et al.[18] introduced a multi-head attention mechanism tailored for multi-sensor prognostics. The PatchTST architecture[19] demonstrated that dividing time series into patches before applying Transformer encoding could improve both computational efficiency and prediction accuracy. However, existing Transformer-based prognostic models remain purely data-driven, lacking the physics-informed regularization necessary to guarantee physically plausible predictions.

E. Digital Twin Frameworks

The digital twin concept, originally proposed by Grieves[20] and formalized by NASA[21], envisions a bidirectional real-time connection between a physical asset and its virtual counterpart. In the context of bearing prognostics, a digital twin must integrate sensor data acquisition, signal processing, model inference, anomaly detection, and maintenance decision support into a cohesive system. Tao et al.[22] proposed a five-dimensional digital twin model encompassing physical entity, virtual entity, services, data, and connections. Piltan and Kim[23] developed a bearing digital twin using a combination of physics models and neural networks. Despite growing interest, few works have demonstrated end-to-end digital twin implementations that extend from raw sensor data to production-grade web-based dashboards with real-time inference capabilities. This paper fills that gap by presenting a complete, deployable digital twin system.

III. Problem Formulation

Consider a bearing operating under steady-state conditions and monitored by n sensor channels sampled at discrete time steps t = 1, 2, …, Tfailure. At each time step, the sensor readings form a feature vector x(t) ∈ ℝn. The remaining useful life at time step t is defined as the number of time steps remaining until the bearing reaches its end-of-life criterion:

RUL(t) = Tfailure − t
(1)

In practice, the raw RUL target is clipped to a maximum value Rmax to form a piecewise-linear degradation target, reflecting the observation that during the early life of a bearing, the exact remaining life is both unknown and irrelevant for maintenance planning:

RULtarget(t) = min(RUL(t), Rmax)
(2)

For this work, we set Rmax = 125 cycles, consistent with established practice in the prognostics literature[24]. The input to the prediction model is a sliding window of w consecutive feature vectors, forming a matrix X(t) ∈ ℝw × n, where w = 30 and n = 14. The objective is to learn a mapping fθ : ℝw × n → ℝ that produces an accurate estimate of RULtarget(t).

We further define the health index as a normalized scalar indicator of bearing condition:

HI(t) = RUL(t)Rmax
(3)

The health index is bounded in [0, 1], where HI = 1.0 indicates nominal condition and HI = 0.0 indicates end-of-life. Bearing degradation is modeled as progressing through three characteristic stages: (i) Normal operation (HI > 0.6), characterized by stable vibration signatures within baseline statistical bounds; (ii) Warning stage (0.25 < HI ≤ 0.6), marked by detectable increases in kurtosis, crest factor, and characteristic defect frequency amplitudes indicating incipient damage; and (iii) Critical stage (HI ≤ 0.25), exhibiting rapidly accelerating degradation with broadband vibration energy increase indicative of advanced spall propagation.

The prediction model must satisfy two physical constraints. First, the monotonicity constraint: for any two time steps t1 < t2 within the same bearing's lifecycle, RUL(t1) ≥ RUL(t2), reflecting the irreversibility of mechanical degradation. Second, the boundary condition: HI(0) ≈ 1.0 at installation and HI(Tfailure) ≈ 0.0 at end-of-life. These constraints are not naturally enforced by standard regression loss functions and must be explicitly incorporated into the training objective.

IV. Methodology

A. Physics Foundation

The physics-informed components of VibFormer are grounded in three fundamental theories of bearing degradation mechanics. We summarize each and derive the corresponding loss terms.

1) Hertzian Contact Stress

The contact between a rolling element and the raceway of a bearing generates localized compressive stress governed by Hertz contact theory. For an elliptical contact patch with semi-axes a and b, the maximum contact stress is given by:

σH = 3F2πab
(4)

where F is the applied normal load, and the contact ellipse dimensions a and b are functions of the elastic moduli and Poisson's ratios of the contacting bodies, the geometrical conformity of the contact, and the load magnitude. For the Rexnord ZA-2115 bearing with AISI 52100 chrome steel rolling elements (Young's modulus E = 210 GPa, Poisson's ratio ν = 0.3), typical maximum contact stresses range from 1.5 to 3.5 GPa under normal operating loads. These stress levels are below the material's ultimate compressive strength but are sufficient to initiate subsurface fatigue microcracking after a sufficient number of stress cycles, a phenomenon known as rolling contact fatigue (RCF).

2) Fatigue Damage Accumulation

The cumulative damage sustained by a bearing under cyclic loading is modeled using the Palmgren–Miner linear damage accumulation rule. If the bearing experiences ni loading cycles at a stress amplitude that would cause failure in Ni cycles (as determined from the material's S-N curve), the total accumulated damage fraction is:

D = Σi niNi
(5)

Failure is predicted when D ≥ 1.0. While the linearity assumption is an idealization—real bearing damage accumulation exhibits load-ordering effects and nonlinear interactions—the Palmgren–Miner rule provides a computationally tractable framework for encoding the physical principle that damage is cumulative and irreversible. In the context of VibFormer, we use the monotonic non-decreasing nature of D(t) to derive the monotonicity constraint on predicted RUL sequences: since accumulated damage can only increase, remaining useful life can only decrease.

3) Crack Propagation (Paris' Law)

Once a fatigue crack has nucleated in the bearing raceway or rolling element, its growth rate under cyclic loading is governed by Paris' law of fatigue crack propagation:

dadN = C · (ΔK)m
(6)

where a is the crack length, N is the number of loading cycles, ΔK is the stress intensity factor range, and C and m are material constants. For AISI 52100 bearing steel, typical values are C ≈ 10−10 m/cycle and m ≈ 3.0. The stress intensity factor range is related to the applied stress amplitude σ and the crack geometry through ΔK = Y·σ·√(πa), where Y is a dimensionless geometry factor. Paris' law provides a physics-based relationship between the observable vibration-derived stress amplitude and the unobservable internal crack growth rate. We encode this relationship as a physics residual loss term: the model is penalized for predictions that imply crack growth rates inconsistent with Paris' law given the observed vibration amplitudes.

B. Transformer Architecture

VibFormer adapts the Transformer encoder architecture for multi-sensor vibration time-series prognostics. The key design insight is to treat contiguous temporal segments of the multi-channel signal as "patches"—analogous to image patches in Vision Transformers—rather than processing individual time steps independently. This patch-based approach reduces the effective sequence length seen by the attention mechanism, improving computational efficiency while preserving temporal resolution within each patch.

1) Patch Projection

Given an input window X ∈ ℝw × n with w = 30 time steps and n = 14 sensor channels, the input is reshaped into P = w/p non-overlapping patches, where p = 5 is the patch size. Each patch is flattened to produce a vector of dimension p · n = 70, which is then linearly projected to the model dimension dmodel = 128:

ei = Wproj · flatten(X[(i−1)p : ip, :]) + bproj
(7)

where Wproj ∈ ℝdmodel × (p·n) and bproj ∈ ℝdmodel are learnable parameters. This yields a sequence of P = 6 patch embeddings, each of dimension 128.

2) Positional Encoding

Learnable positional embeddings Epos ∈ ℝP × dmodel are added to the patch embeddings to inject temporal ordering information. We chose learnable (as opposed to sinusoidal) positional encodings because vibration data lacks the strict sequential semantics of natural language, and learnable embeddings allow the model to discover the optimal temporal weighting from data. The positioned embeddings are:

z0 = [e1 + Epos,1; …; eP + Epos,P]
(8)

3) Multi-Head Self-Attention

The core computational mechanism of VibFormer is scaled dot-product self-attention. For an input sequence of embeddings Z ∈ ℝP × dmodel, the queries, keys, and values are obtained through linear projections:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKT√dk) · V
(9)

where dk = dmodel/h = 16 is the per-head dimension and h = 8 is the number of attention heads. No causal mask is applied because health estimation is not an autoregressive task—the model is free to attend to all time positions within the window. The multi-head formulation enables different heads to capture different temporal interaction patterns:

MultiHead(Q, K, V) = Concat(head1, …, headh) · WO
(10)

where headi = Attention(ZWiQ, ZWiK, ZWiV) and WO ∈ ℝdmodel × dmodel is the output projection.

4) Feed-Forward Network and Encoder Block

Each Transformer encoder layer consists of a multi-head self-attention sublayer followed by a position-wise feed-forward network (FFN), with pre-layer normalization and residual connections for training stability. The FFN applies two linear transformations with a GELU activation:

FFN(z) = GELU(zW1 + b1) · W2 + b2
(11)

where W1 ∈ ℝdmodel × 4dmodel and W2 ∈ ℝ4dmodel × dmodel provide a bottleneck expansion of factor 4. VibFormer uses L = 4 stacked encoder layers. After the final encoder layer, global average pooling aggregates the P patch representations into a single vector of dimension dmodel, which is then passed to a two-layer MLP regression head with ReLU output activation to enforce non-negativity of predicted RUL values. The total model comprises approximately 340,000 trainable parameters.

C. Physics-Informed Loss Function

The total training loss is a weighted combination of three complementary objectives. The data loss measures the standard prediction error between the model output and the ground-truth RUL target using mean squared error:

ℒdata = 1N Σi=1N (ŷi − yi)2
(12)

The physics residual loss penalizes deviations from Paris' law by comparing the observed vibration-derived stress amplitude proxy (mean sensor reading) against the expected crack growth rate:

ℒphysics = 1N Σi=1N (ȧi − C · σim)2
(13)

The monotonicity loss penalizes any increase in predicted RUL between consecutive time steps within a batch sequence, directly encoding the physical principle that accumulated damage is irreversible:

ℒmono = 1N − 1 Σi=1N−1 max(0, ŷi+1 − ŷi)2
(14)

The total physics-informed loss is:

ℒtotal = ℒdata + λ1 · ℒphysics + λ2 · ℒmono
(15)

where λ1 = 0.1 and λ2 = 0.05 are hyperparameters selected via grid search on the validation set. The physics and monotonicity losses act as soft constraints: they do not rigidly enforce the physical laws but penalize deviations, allowing the model to gracefully handle noise and model mismatch while maintaining approximate physical consistency. This "physics as regularizer" approach avoids the brittleness of full mechanistic models while producing outputs that are interpretable by domain engineers.

Fig. 4. Decomposition of the physics-informed loss function into three components. The total loss ℒtotal combines data-driven MSE loss with a Paris' law physics residual (penalizing crack-growth rates inconsistent with observed stress amplitudes) and a monotonicity penalty (penalizing non-decreasing RUL predictions), weighted by λ₁ = 0.1 and λ₂ = 0.05 respectively.

D. Digital Twin Pipeline

The VibFormer model is deployed within a complete digital twin architecture that enables real-time monitoring and predictive maintenance of a fleet of four bearings. The pipeline operates in four stages:

Stage 1 — Data Ingestion: Raw vibration data from accelerometers mounted on each bearing housing is acquired at 20 kHz and segmented into 10-minute measurement windows. Each raw window undergoes bandpass filtering (1–10 kHz), z-score normalization, and artifact rejection to remove transient electrical interference. Fourteen time-domain and frequency-domain features are extracted from each window: root mean square (RMS) amplitude, kurtosis, skewness, crest factor, peak-to-peak amplitude, spectral entropy, and amplitudes at the eight characteristic bearing defect frequencies (BPFO, BPFI, BSF, and their harmonics).

Stage 2 — Model Inference: The extracted feature windows are fed to the VibFormer model, which has been exported to ONNX format and optimized with ONNX Runtime for low-latency CPU inference. The model produces a scalar RUL prediction for each bearing at each time step, with inference latency of approximately 3.2 ms per window on a single CPU core, or 0.9 ms with INT8 quantization—well within the real-time budget for 10-minute measurement intervals.

Stage 3 — Anomaly Attribution and Health Classification: The predicted RUL is normalized to a health index HI ∈ [0, 1] and compared against configurable thresholds to classify each bearing into one of three operational states: NORMAL (HI > 0.6), WARNING (0.25 < HI ≤ 0.6), or CRITICAL (HI ≤ 0.25). State transitions trigger automatic alerts that are persisted to a SQLite database for audit trail purposes. Attention weight visualization provides interpretability, enabling engineers to identify which temporal segments contributed most to the degradation assessment.

Stage 4 — Dashboard and Visualization: The FastAPI backend serves a React frontend via HTTP and streams real-time telemetry updates to connected clients via WebSocket connections. Each bearing has a dedicated WebSocket channel (/ws/telemetry/B1 through /ws/telemetry/B4). The dashboard features include: (i) real-time health index gauges for all four bearings, (ii) historical RUL trend charts with predicted vs. true RUL overlays, (iii) 3D bearing visualization rendered with Three.js showing color-coded component health, (iv) alert history with acknowledgment workflow, and (v) CSV export of telemetry data for offline analysis.

V. System Architecture

The end-to-end system architecture integrates data acquisition, signal processing, model inference, data persistence, and interactive visualization into a containerized, production-deployable stack. VI. Dataset and Experimental Setup

A. IMS Bearing Dataset

Experiments are conducted on the IMS (Intelligent Maintenance Systems) bearing dataset from the University of Cincinnati, hosted by NASA's Prognostics Center of Excellence[24]. The dataset comprises three separate run-to-failure experiments on a bearing test rig equipped with four Rexnord ZA-2115 double-row bearings loaded with a radial force of 6,000 lbs (26.7 kN), operating at a constant shaft speed of 2,000 rpm. Each bearing is monitored by two accelerometers (horizontal and vertical channels), yielding 8 channels per test run. Vibration data is sampled at 20,480 Hz with each measurement window capturing 20,480 data points (1 second of data), acquired at 10-minute intervals throughout the bearing's operational life.

B. Feature Extraction and Preprocessing

For each 1-second vibration snapshot, 14 time-domain and frequency-domain features are extracted per channel. Time-domain features include: RMS amplitude, kurtosis, skewness, crest factor, peak-to-peak value, standard deviation, and impulse factor. Frequency-domain features include amplitudes at the bearing characteristic defect frequencies—ball pass frequency outer race (BPFO), ball pass frequency inner race (BPFI), ball spin frequency (BSF), and fundamental train frequency (FTF)—along with their second harmonics and spectral entropy. All features are z-score normalized per channel using statistics computed on the training partition.

Sliding windows of 30 consecutive time steps are generated with a stride of 1, yielding input tensors of shape (30, 14). The piecewise-linear RUL target is computed for each window by clipping the true remaining time-to-failure at Rmax = 125 cycles. The dataset is partitioned with Test Runs 1 and 2 used for training and validation (80/20 random split stratified by bearing), and Test Run 3 held out entirely as the test set to evaluate generalization across independent run-to-failure experiments.

D. Baselines

We compare VibFormer against four baseline methods:

  • LSTM: A two-layer LSTM with 128 hidden units followed by a fully connected regression head, trained with MSE loss. This represents the standard recurrent baseline for time-series prognostics.
  • CNN: A 1D convolutional network with three convolutional blocks (32, 64, 128 filters, kernel size 3) followed by global average pooling and a fully connected layer. This baseline captures local temporal patterns without explicit long-range modeling.
  • MLP: A multi-layer perceptron that flattens the input window and processes it through three hidden layers (256, 128, 64 units) with ReLU activations. This baseline lacks any sequential inductive bias.
  • Traditional (Statistical): A support vector regression model with RBF kernel applied to hand-crafted statistical features (same 14 features used by VibFormer, but without temporal windowing). This represents the pre-deep-learning state of practice.

All neural network baselines use the same 14-feature input representation, identical train/val/test partitions, and are trained with the same AdamW optimizer configuration and early stopping criteria for fair comparison. Only VibFormer includes the physics-informed loss components; all baselines are trained with standard MSE loss.

First, VibFormer (teal line) closely follows the ground-truth trajectory with minimal deviation, producing a smooth, monotonically decreasing prediction curve consistent with physical degradation dynamics. Second, the 95% confidence band (shaded region) is narrow and calibrated, tightening as the bearing approaches failure—consistent with the intuition that degradation signals become more pronounced and unambiguous in later life stages. Third, the LSTM baseline exhibits characteristic oscillatory behavior in the mid-degradation regime (time steps 300–600), periodically predicting RUL increases that violate monotonicity. This is precisely the failure mode that the physics-informed loss is designed to suppress. Fourth, the CNN baseline shows systematic over-prediction in the critical late-life region (time steps 650+), which would lead to dangerously late maintenance interventions in a real deployment.

The ablation results in Table IV and Figure 7 reveal several important findings. Removing the physics loss (ℒphysics) increases RMSE by 10.4% (from 12.87 to 14.21), confirming that Paris' law regularization provides meaningful guidance during training beyond what the data alone can provide. Removing the monotonicity penalty (ℒmono) has a smaller impact on RMSE (5.4% increase) but dramatically reduces the monotonicity score from 0.97 to 0.84, indicating that the model produces 16% more physically inconsistent predictions—a substantial degradation in the trustworthiness of the system for deployment in safety-critical settings. Removing self-attention (replacing the Transformer encoder with a simple feed-forward network applied to each patch independently) causes the largest performance drop (27.6% RMSE increase), confirming that the ability to model temporal interactions between patches is essential for capturing the evolving degradation dynamics. Finally, removing patching (processing individual time steps as tokens) causes a moderate 8.3% RMSE increase, validating the patch-based approach as a beneficial inductive bias for this domain.

VIII. Discussion

A. Strengths

The VibFormer framework demonstrates several advantages over existing approaches. First, the physics-informed loss function effectively bridges the gap between purely data-driven and physics-based methods, achieving the predictive accuracy of deep learning while maintaining the physical interpretability demanded by industrial end-users. The monotonicity score of 0.97 is a particularly important practical outcome: maintenance engineers are far more likely to trust and act upon RUL predictions that follow physically intuitive degradation trajectories, even if a small amount of predictive accuracy is sacrificed for this consistency.

Second, the Transformer architecture's self-attention mechanism offers inherent interpretability through attention weight analysis. Unlike LSTM hidden states, which encode information in opaque fixed-dimensional vectors, attention weights directly reveal which temporal segments the model considers most informative for degradation assessment. In our experiments, we observed that attention consistently concentrated on patches containing sharp kurtosis increases and spectral energy redistribution events—precisely the signatures that domain experts associate with bearing fault initiation and progression.

Third, the end-to-end digital twin deployment demonstrates that physics-informed deep learning models can be integrated into production-grade monitoring systems without sacrificing real-time performance. The ONNX Runtime inference latency of 3.2 ms per window (0.9 ms with INT8 quantization) leaves substantial headroom for the 10-minute measurement intervals typical of industrial bearing monitoring, enabling deployment on edge computing hardware without GPU requirements. The WebSocket-based streaming architecture scales efficiently with the number of monitored bearings, supporting concurrent fleet monitoring with minimal server resource overhead.

B. Limitations

Several limitations merit discussion. First, VibFormer has been trained and evaluated exclusively on the IMS bearing dataset under controlled laboratory conditions (constant speed, constant load). Transfer to operational environments with variable speed profiles, fluctuating loads, and different bearing geometries would require domain adaptation techniques—potentially fine-tuning with target-domain data or unsupervised domain adaptation methods. Cross-dataset evaluation on FEMTO/PRONOSTIA and XJTU-SY bearing datasets is planned for future work.

Second, the physics-informed loss components encode simplified models of bearing degradation. Paris' law assumes linear elastic fracture mechanics in a homogeneous material, while real bearing steels exhibit nonlinear crack-tip plasticity, inclusion-driven crack nucleation, and microstructural heterogeneity. The Palmgren–Miner rule's linearity assumption ignores load-ordering and interaction effects. These simplifications are intentional—the goal is physics as regularizer, not physics as simulator—but they constrain the model's ability to capture complex multi-mode failure scenarios.

Third, the health index is a learned latent proxy for degradation severity, not a directly measurable physical quantity. While it correlates strongly with expert-assessed bearing condition (as evidenced by the 94.2% classification accuracy), the absolute RUL values should be treated as ordinal rankings rather than precise calendar-time predictions. Calibrating the RUL output to maintenance-actionable time horizons requires site-specific validation against historical failure records.

Fourth, the monotonicity constraint is enforced as a soft penalty rather than a hard architectural constraint. While the 0.97 monotonicity score represents a significant improvement over unconstrained baselines (0.81), rare violations can still occur under severe distributional shift. Future work could explore hard monotonicity enforcement through isotonic regression post-processing or constrained optimization layers.

C. Deployment Considerations

Deploying VibFormer in an industrial setting requires attention to several practical considerations. First, sensor installation quality directly impacts prediction reliability: accelerometer mounting resonance, cable impedance mismatches, and electromagnetic interference from variable-frequency drives can introduce artifacts that the model has not been trained to handle. Pre-deployment signal quality validation and automated artifact detection are essential prerequisites.

Second, the model requires periodic retraining or online adaptation to account for fleet-wide variations in bearing installation quality, lubrication conditions, and alignment accuracy. A Bayesian online learning extension that updates model parameters incrementally as new run-to-failure data becomes available would reduce the maintenance burden of model lifecycle management.

Third, alert fatigue is a significant concern in fleet monitoring systems. The configurable health index thresholds (0.6 for Warning, 0.25 for Critical) should be calibrated to the specific operational context: a conservative threshold reduces missed detections but increases false alarms, while an aggressive threshold reduces alert volume at the risk of missing early-stage degradation. Adaptive threshold optimization using reinforcement learning or Bayesian decision theory represents a promising direction for future work.

IX. Conclusion and Future Work

This paper has presented VibFormer, a physics-informed digital twin framework for remaining useful life prediction of industrial bearings. By combining a patch-based Transformer encoder with a composite loss function that integrates data-driven MSE, Paris' law crack-propagation residuals, and monotonicity constraints derived from Palmgren–Miner fatigue theory, VibFormer achieves state-of-the-art prediction accuracy while maintaining physically consistent degradation trajectories. On the IMS bearing dataset, VibFormer achieves an RMSE of 12.87 cycles, outperforming LSTM baselines by 18.2% and traditional methods by 39.1%, with a monotonicity score of 0.97 that ensures predictions are trustworthy for industrial deployment. The complete digital twin system—spanning ONNX-optimized inference, FastAPI backend, WebSocket streaming, SQLite persistence, and React/Three.js dashboard—demonstrates that physics-informed deep learning can be deployed in production-grade monitoring infrastructure without compromising real-time performance.

Several directions for future work are planned. First, uncertainty quantification through conformal prediction will provide calibrated prediction intervals around RUL estimates, enabling risk-aware maintenance decision-making. Second, online Bayesian adaptation will allow the model to update its parameters incrementally as new run-to-failure data becomes available, reducing the need for periodic offline retraining. Third, multi-asset fleet modeling with hierarchical Bayesian priors will enable information sharing across bearings with similar degradation profiles, improving prediction accuracy for newly installed bearings with limited operational history. Fourth, cross-dataset evaluation on FEMTO/PRONOSTIA, XJTU-SY, and C-MAPSS datasets will assess the generalizability of the physics-informed approach across different bearing types, failure modes, and operating conditions. Fifth, systematic benchmarking against contemporary architectures—including Informer, Autoformer, PatchTST, and temporal convolutional networks—at matched computational budgets will position VibFormer relative to the rapidly evolving landscape of time-series Transformer variants. Finally, integration of vibration-based root cause analysis through attention weight interpretation and gradient-based attribution will enhance the diagnostic utility of the system, enabling engineers to not only predict when a bearing will fail but understand the underlying physical mechanism driving its degradation.
